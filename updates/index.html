● Signed order · 15 July 2026
Miami-Dade · Case 2026-001147-CA-01 · Filing # 252525224

The written summary judgment order is signed and entered

On 15 July 2026, Circuit Court Judge Spencer Eig signed and entered the written Order Granting Plaintiffs' Motion for Summary Judgment against Secondlife Electronics Trading LLC (Case No. 2026-001147-CA-01, Filing # 252525224). What was granted from the bench on 15 May is now a signed order on the record.

The findings, in the Court's words

The Settlement Agreement of 4 June 2025 is “a valid and binding contract, as Defendant has admitted.” Secondlife was required to pay $1,500,000 in monthly installments; it paid only $300,000 “and has admitted that fact.” The Court found the remaining balance to be $1,200,000.

The defense was rejected

Secondlife's sole evidence in opposition was the Tothfalussy Declaration. The Court found it “does not assert agency as a fact” but “asserts only suspicion, perception, and belief,” which is “not competent summary judgment evidence.” The Court noted that the 13 March 2025 letter from Secondlife's own counsel to the Miami Police Department — stating counsel had “no evidence” the lenders were involved and no “proof” that Mr. Pichler was involved — “negates any inference that Plaintiffs directed or controlled Pichler's alleged conduct.” Its conclusion: “no reasonable trier of fact could draw the inference of agency.”

The ruling

“Plaintiffs' Motion for Summary Judgment is GRANTED. There is no legitimate, genuine dispute over any material fact in this action... Plaintiffs are entitled to judgment as a matter of law.”

What comes next

The Court directed the lenders to file a separate motion for entry of a final money judgment of $1,200,000 plus pre-judgment interest, with reasonable attorneys' fees and costs awarded to the lenders as the prevailing party. The order states that no further judicial action is required on the motion and directs the clerk to reclose the case if post-judgment.

The signed order is on the public docket. Filing # 252525224 · Case No. 2026-001147-CA-01 · Hon. Spencer Eig

“Suspicion, perception, and belief are not competent summary judgment evidence.”

The discovery that did not happen. Florida's summary judgment rule (1.510(d)) allows a defendant who needs more time to gather evidence to ask the Court for a continuance to do discovery first. Secondlife's counsel did not ask. The Reply, in its own words: “more than a month after Plaintiffs' Motion was filed, Secondlife still has not initiated any discovery.”

A defendant whose entire defense rests on the proposition that the author of this site is the lenders' agent, and who could have asked the Court for time to find documentary evidence of that agency, did not ask.

The reader may draw an inference.

Two further arguments are also in the Reply, noted briefly: that paragraph 9(a) of the Settlement Agreement waives all defenses on monetary default, and that creditors waiting on installment payments do not rationally sabotage their own debtor. Both are arguments for the Court.

Miami-Dade · Case 2026-003865-CA-01

Motion to Dismiss granted in Sunderland et al. v. Andras Tothfalussy

The court has dismissed the complaint filed by Stuart Sunderland, Henry Robert Heneage, Hamish Blair and James Way against András Tothfalussy.

The dismissal was granted purely on procedural grounds due to several basic drafting errors by the plaintiffs’ lawyer:

  • Classic “shotgun” group pleading (lumping all four plaintiffs and claims together instead of separate counts for each lender)
  • Failure to attach the actual Loan Agreements and the Settlement Agreement
  • Fraud claims not pled with the required particularity (“who, what, when, where, how”)

The judge did not rule on the merits of the fraud allegations. The plaintiffs have been given leave to file an amended complaint to correct these technical mistakes.

This does not mean the underlying claims against him have been rejected.

Note

The vast majority of civil fraud cases in Florida never get a final “win” or “loss” at trial. They are either settled (most common outcome), or dismissed early (like the motion to dismiss you just saw). Only 1–3% of civil cases ever reach a full trial (jury or bench).
